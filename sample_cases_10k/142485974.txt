                 "In the case of a discretionary       presumption the
                 presumption     if drawn may be rebutted by an
                 explanation which "might reasonably be true and which
                 is consistent with the innocence" of the accused. On
                 the other hand in the case of a mandatory presumption
                 "the burden resting on the accused person in such a
                 case would not be as light as it is where a presumption
                 is raised under <a href="/doc/731516/" id="a_1">Section 114</a> of the Evidence Act and
                 cannot be held to be discharged merely by reason of
                 the fact that the explanation offered by the accused is
                 reasonable and probable". It must further be shown that
                 the explanation is a true one. The words 'unless the
                 contrary is proved'      which occur in this provision
                 (<a href="/doc/371095/" id="a_2">Sections 118</a> and <a href="/doc/334170/" id="a_3">139</a> of the Act) make it clear that the
                 presumption has to be rebutted by 'proof' and not by a
                 bare explanation which is merely plausible. A fact is
                 said to be proved when its existence is directly
                 established or when upon the material before it the
                 Court finds its existence to be so probable that a
                 reasonable man would act on the supposition that it
                 exists. Unless, the explanation is supported by proof,
                 the mandatory presumption created by the provisions
                 cannot be said to be rebutted."

<p id="p_13">      IN THE COURT OF SH. VIVEK KUMAR GULIA METROPOLITAN
                  MAGISTRATE : KARKARDOOMA COURTS : DELHI


                                                                                CC No. 7420/07

Rani Jain                                   Vs.                                Zakir Hussain

                                     Order on sentence

Present :-          Complainant in person.
</p><p id="p_14">                    Accused with counsel Sh. Sanjay Sharma.
</p>
<p id="p_15">             Complainant contended that since convict issued the cheque in
discharge of the liability and his guilt has been established beyond any doubt, he
be punished with maximum sentence provided.
</p>
<p id="p_16">              On the other hand, convict submits that as he is first offender and also
sole bread winner of his family, a lenient view view may be taken. It is also stated
by convict that he is ready to compensate the complainant in addition to making
payment of cheque amount. On this, it is stated on behalf of complainant that she
will be satisfied if convict is directed to make payment of Rs. 2,36,000/- to
complainant out of which Rs. 25,000/- has already been paid to the complainant as
litigation charges on 18.12.2008. Today an amount of Rs. 31,000/- is paid to the
complainant in cash, remaining settlement amount Rs. 1,80,000/- shall be paid in
twelve equal monthly installments w.e.f. 18.02.2009. Post dated cheque nos.
292512 to 292522 &amp; last cheque no. 292511 dated 18.02.2009 to 18.01.2010
respectively handed over to complainant.
</p>
<p id="p_17">              In such circumstances, when the parties have settled the dispute and
convict is ready to make the payment of settlement amount, no useful purpose will
be served to send the convict behind bars. Accordingly, as per <a href="/doc/259863/" id="a_4">section 143(1)</a>
(proviso) <a href="/doc/1132672/" id="a_5">N.I. Act</a> r/w <a href="/doc/1365288/" id="a_6">section 357(1)(b)</a> of Cr.P.C., convict is sentenced to fine of
Rs. 2,36,000/- which will be paid to complainant as compensation as per aforesaid
payment schedule. In default of payment of fine or compensation, convict shall
under go simple imprisonment for three months.
</p>
