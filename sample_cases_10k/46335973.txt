<div class="doc_title">Aziz Mondal &amp; Anr vs Oriental Insurance Co. Ltd. &amp; Anr on 28 June, 2018</div><pre id="pre_1">                              1



28.06.2018
127.ML. AJ.

               C.A.N. 9172 of 2016
                                in
                         F.M.A.T. 766 of 2016

                          Aziz Mondal &amp; Anr.
                                  -Vs-
                   Oriental Insurance Co. Ltd. &amp; Anr.
              Mr. Muktakesh Das.
                    .......for the appellants/claimants.

              Mr. Rajesh Singh.
                     .......for the respondent no. 1/</pre>
<p id="p_1">                          insurance co.
</p>
<p id="p_2">       C.A.N. 9172 of 2016 is an application under <a href="/doc/100581/" id="a_1">Section

5</a> of the Limitation Act seeking condonation of 97 days'

delay in presentation of F.M.A.T. 766 of 2016, being an

appeal under <a href="/doc/147367599/" id="a_2">Section 173</a> of the Motor Vehicles Act, 1988.

</p><p id="p_3">       The appeal is directed against an award dated 15th

January, 2016 passed by the Motor Accident Claims

Tribunal cum District Judge, Nadia at Krishnagar in

M.A.C. Case No. 398 of 2010 (hereafter the 'tribunal').

Although the length of delay is not at all shocking, we do

not see reason to exercise discretion and thereby condone

such delay in presentation of the appeal for the reason that

follows.
</p>
<p id="p_4">       The         tribunal       awarded   Rs.1,62,500/-   as

compensation. The insurance company was directed to
<span class="hidden_text" id="span_1">                        2</span>


make payment of such compensation within 60 days,

failing which such sum shall carry interest @ 9% per

annum.
</p>
<p id="p_5">        To avoid payment of interest, the insurance company

satisfied   the   award.   The   appellants   received   the

compensation amount without raising any protest. After

receiving the same, the appeal has been presented beyond

time.
</p>
<p id="p_6">        Law is well settled that the length of delay in

presentation of an appeal is not relevant for deciding

whether the delay should be condoned or not; what is

relevant is the cause shown. A long delay could be

condoned provided sufficient cause is shown, whereas a

few days' delay may not be condoned if sufficient cause

were not shown. Here, we have a case where the appellants

seek condonation of only 97 days' delay.       Delay, it is

pleaded, was caused because of their ignorance of the legal

provisions. They have not disclosed in the application that

they received the sum on account of compensation,

awarded by the tribunal. Having regard to their conduct in

receiving the compensation awarded by the tribunal

without any mark of protest, we are of the considered view

that exercise of discretionary power, on facts and in the
<span class="hidden_text" id="span_2">                        3</span>


circumstances, would not be proper. The conduct of the

appellants is such that they are not entitled to exercise of

discretion in their favour.
</p>
<p id="p_7">      For the reasons aforesaid, the application under

<a href="/doc/100581/" id="a_3">Section 5</a> of the Limitation Act stands dismissed.

</p><p id="p_8">      There shall, however, be no order as to costs.

      Urgent photostat certified copy of this order, if

applied for, be supplied expeditiously after complying with

all necessary legal formalities.
</p>
<p id="p_9">


</p><p id="p_10">                                ( Dipankar Datta, J. )




                              ( Rajarshi Bharadwaj, J. )
