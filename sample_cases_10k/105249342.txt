<p id="p_1">1.        Vide this common judgment, I shall dispose off five appeals
instituted   on   23.06.2016   by   the   appellant/   convict   Sanjay   Shah
whereby  the  appellant   has  assailed   the  impugned  judgments  dated
21.04.2016 and orders on sentence dated 16.05.2016 passed by Ms
Aakanksha Vyas, Ld. MM (N.I. Act), West, Delhi in five Complaint
Cases bearing CC NO.4380/1/12,   4379/1/12, 4378/1/12, 4381/1/12
&amp;   4382/1/12   respectively   under   Section   138   of   the   Negotiable
Instruments Act (in short 'N.I. Act').
</p><p id="p_2">          Vide   this   common   judgment,   I   shall   also   dispose   off   the
criminal   appeal   bearing   CA   NO.54499/16,   preferred   by   the
complainant/ respondent herein assailing the orders on sentence dated
16.05.2016 on the ground that lesser punishment has been awarded to
the   accused,   which   is   sought   to   be   enhanced   and   grant   of

CA No.54458­62/16 &amp; 54499/16          Sanjay Shah vs. Narender Budhiraja                              Page2of17
 compensation   to   the   extent   of   double   the   cheques   amount   is   also
prayed.
</p><p id="p_3">          The   appeal   filed   by   the   complainant   is   accompanied   by   an
application for condonation of delay in filing the same. In view of
reasons stated in the application, the said application is allowed and
the appeal is heard on merits in the interest of justice..</p><pre id="pre_2">


2.        For the sake of convenience, the particulars of each case are
drawn in tabular form which are as under :­

</pre><p id="p_4">CA No.54458­62/16 &amp; 54499/16          Sanjay Shah vs. Narender Budhiraja                              Page3of17
</p><p id="p_5"> 3.        The   case   of   the   complainant   in   all   the   aforesaid   complaint
cases is that on 22.10.2011, he advanced a loan of Rs.5,24,000/­ to
the appellant and appellant assured to repay the same by 20.01.2012
for which an affidavit was executed by him. For repayment of the
said   loan,   appellant   issued   five   post   dated   cheques   as   aforesaid.
However,   upon   presentation,   all   the   aforesaid   five   cheques   were
dishonoured on account of insufficient funds. Hence, the complainant
demanded  the cheques  amount as aforesaid  by legal notices dated
01.02.2012 but no payment was made by the appellant. Having no
alternative, the complainant filed the aforesaid complaint cases.
</p>

<p id="p_6">4.        The   appellant   prayed   for   his   acquittal   by   assailing   the
impugned judgments and orders on sentence interalia pleading that :­


      •   Ld. Trial  Court  failed  to consider  the defence raised by the
          appellant that he had borrowed a sum of Rs.20,000/­ only from
          the   complainant   which   was   agreed   to   be   repayable   in   daily
          installments of Rs.240/­ per day;
</p><p id="p_7">      •   Ld. Trial Court failed to consider the defence of the appellant
          that   he   had   already   paid   68   installments   (Rs.240/­   per   day)
          receipt   of   which   amount   was   acknowledged   by   the
          complainant vide document duly proved as Ex.DW1/A;
      •   Ld.  Trial   Court   ignored  the   sanctity  of   document  proved   as
          Ex.DW1/A vide which respondent in his own handwriting and
CA No.54458­62/16 &amp; 54499/16          Sanjay Shah vs. Narender Budhiraja                              Page4of17
           under   his   signatures   acknowledged   that   he   had   received   an
          amount   of   Rs.240/­   per   day   from   the   appellant   towards
          liquidation of the loan of Rs.20,000/­ only;
      •   Ld. Trial Court failed to appreciate the defence of the appellant
          that the cheques in question bear his signatures but he had not
          filled in other particulars in the cheques which cheques were
          issued as security against the loan amount of Rs.20,000/­ only;
      •   Ld. Trial Court failed to appreciate the fact that complainant
          failed   to   disclose   the   source   from   where   he   arranged   to
          advance the loan amount;
</p><p id="p_8">      •   Ld. Trial Court failed to consider the fact that the stamp paper
          was   purchased   in   the   year   2010   and   the   same   was   used   in
          January 2012 for preparation of affidavit qua loan transaction
          and that complainant failed to explain the long gap;
      •   Ld. Trial Court failed to consider the fact that particulars of the
          cheques   in   question   do   not   find   mention   in   the   affidavit
          Ex.CW1/A;
</p>

<p id="p_9">5.        TCRs   of   aforesaid   five   complaint   cases   were   requisitioned
which have been received and perused. I have heard Ld. counsel for
the appellant and the complainant.
</p>

<p id="p_10">6.        Perusal of TCRs shows that on appreciation of pre­summoning
evidence   recorded   under   Section   200   <a href="/doc/445276/" id="a_1">Cr.P.C</a>.,   the   appellant   was
CA No.54458­62/16 &amp; 54499/16          Sanjay Shah vs. Narender Budhiraja                              Page5of17
 summoned and he was served with notice under Section 251 <a href="/doc/445276/" id="a_2">Cr.P.C</a>.
on 01.03.2014 in all  the complaint cases to which he pleaded not
guilty and claimed trial.
</p><p id="p_11">          The appellant while replying to notice u/s 251 <a href="/doc/445276/" id="a_3">Cr.P.C</a>. in all the
complaint cases, clearly admitted that the five cheques in question
bear his signatures. He, however, added that he did not fill­in the
particulars. 
</p><p id="p_12">          Since, the appellant himself admitted that he issued the cheques
in question, presumption under Sections 118 and 139 of N.I. Act can
be   drawn,  placing   reliance   on   the   judgment   of   the   Hon'ble   Apex
Court in  <a href="/doc/150051/" id="a_4">Rangappa vs. Mohan</a> AIR 2010 SC 1989  which is to the
effect that :­
                    "Once the cheque relates to the account of the accused
                    and  he accepts and admits the signatures on the said
                    cheque,   then   initial   presumption   as   contemplated
                    under Section 139 of the Negotiable Instruments Act
                    has   to   be   raised   by   the   Court   in   favour   of   the
                    respondent."
</p>


<p id="p_13">7.        The plea of the appellant that he did not fill in other particulars
in the cheques in question can be discarded by placing reliance on the
judgment of Hon'ble High Court in case  Ravi Chopra Vs. State &amp;
Anr., Crl. M.C. 5211/2006 and Crl. M.A. No. 8864/2006, wherein it
was held that:
</p><p id="p_14">                    "where the accused facing the trial for the offence u/s
                    138 is disputing the signature on the cheque  itself,

CA No.54458­62/16 &amp; 54499/16          Sanjay Shah vs. Narender Budhiraja                              Page6of17
                     then this is a permissible defence within the scope of
                    Section 138 of NI Act but where the signature are
                    admitted,  although   the   cheques   are   issued  in   blank
                    there is an implied authority given to the payee to fill
                    up the instrument. Section 20 of NI Act also say so."
</p>


<p id="p_15">          Reliance in this regard may also be placed on the Judgment of
Hon'ble High Court in <a href="/doc/324041/" id="a_5">Jaipal Singh Rana vs. Swaraj Pal</a>, 149 (2008)
DLT 882  wherein it was observed that there is no law that requires
that particulars of the entire cheque should be filled in by the drawer
himself and further on the judgment of Hon'ble High Court of Delhi
in  <a href="/doc/143232225/" id="a_6">Vijender   Singh   vs.   Eicher   Motors   Limited   &amp;   Anrs</a>.     Crl.   M.C.
1454/2011 decided on 05.05.2011  wherein it was observed that any
person   who   issues   blank   signed   cheque   should   understand   the
consequences of doing so.
</p><p id="p_16">          The   appellant   having   himself   admitted   that   he   signed   the
cheques in question and by handing over the cheques in question to
the complainant gave him express and implied authority to fill in his
name as payee. 
</p>

<p id="p_17">8.        Now, having drawn presumption in favour of the complainant
in terms of judgment of Hon'ble Apex Court in  <a href="/doc/150051/" id="a_7">Rangappa vs. Mohan</a>
(supra), onus is shifted to the appellant to create a dent in the case of
the complainant. 
</p>


<p id="p_18">CA No.54458­62/16 &amp; 54499/16          Sanjay Shah vs. Narender Budhiraja                              Page7of17
</p><p id="p_19"> 9.        Ld.   counsel   for   the   appellant   vehemently   contended   that
document   Ex.DW1/A   shows   that   appellant   had   already   made   the
payments as reflected therein and this document indicates that loan
was duly liquidated but the complainant with a view to extort more
money from the complainant, did not return the security cheques and
filed the aforesaid complaint cases.
</p>

<p id="p_20">10.       Ld.   Trial   Court   dealt   with   the   said   contention   and   rightly
observed in the impugned judgments which is reproduced for ready
reference as under :­
                  "I now come to Ex.DW1/A i.e. the document placed
                on record by the accused. It is pertinent to note that
                neither in his statement under section 251 or during
                the   cross­examination   of   the   complainant   or   in   his
                statement   under   section   313   <a href="/doc/445276/" id="a_8">CrPC</a>   or   in   his
                application under section 145(2) of the NI Act, any
                mention has been made of this document. This fact is
                disconcerting as this document purportedly contains
                an acknowledgement of the complainant with respect
                to the loan amount allegedly taken by the accused in
                the sum of Rs.20,000/­ only and payments of daily
                installments   of   Rs.240/­   made   by   the   accused.   The
                record reflects that this document came to light for
                the first time during the examination in chief of the
                accused. The complainant has denied his signatures
                on this document or the preparation of this document.
                Under section 67 of the Indian Evidence Act 1872,
                where a document is alleged to be signed or written
                by   a   person,   the   signature   and   handwriting   of   that
                person must be proved. No other evidence has been
                led by the accused to establish the same in respect of
                Ex.DW1/A.   Viewed   in   the   totality   of   the

CA No.54458­62/16 &amp; 54499/16          Sanjay Shah vs. Narender Budhiraja                              Page8of17
                 circumstances   in   which   this   document   has   been
                brought   on   record,   this   document   cannot   be   relied
                upon."
</p>

<p id="p_21">11.       Further, it is also reflected from the TCRs that appellant did not
reply  to   the  legal   demand   notices   of   the   complainant  although   he
admitted   in   reply   to   notice   under   Section   251   <a href="/doc/445276/" id="a_9">CrPC</a>   that   he   had
received the said legal demand notices. So, Ld. Trial Court rightly
observed that :­
               "the   conduct   of   the   accused   in   not   replying   to   the
               notices   of   the   complainant   is   a   relevant   fact   and
               entitles the court to draw a presumption under <a href="/doc/989759/" id="a_10">section
               114</a> of the Indian Evidence Act that there is a truth in
               the averments of the complainant. Reliance is placed
               upon the decision of the Supreme Court in <a href="/doc/150051/" id="a_11">Rangappa
               v.   Sri   Mohan</a>  (judgment   of   Supreme   Court   dated
               07.05.2010   in   Criminal   Appeal   No.1020   of   2010),
               where it has been held that :­
                      "the very fact that the accused had failed to
                      reply to the statutory notice under <a href="/doc/1834702/" id="a_12">Section
                      138</a>   of   the   Act   lead   to   the   inference   that
                      there   was   merit   in   the   complainant's
                      version."
</p>

<p id="p_22">          It   is   relevant   to   note   that   as   per   case   of   the   appellant   he
borrowed a petty amount of Rs.20,000/­ only from the complainant.
Admittedly, he signed five cheques in favour of the complainant. It
does not stand to reason as to why for a small amount of Rs.20,000/­
only, the appellant would have issued five cheques. It further does not
stand   to   reason   as   to   why   the   cheques   were   signed   at   all   by   the

CA No.54458­62/16 &amp; 54499/16          Sanjay Shah vs. Narender Budhiraja                              Page9of17
 appellant when as per his own case, he had discharged his liability
vide Ex.DW1/A. There is nothing on record that the appellant had
ever written to the complainant that he had returned the loan amount
to the complainant. There is nothing on record to prove that appellant
ever   mentioned to  the complainant  that as  he had  repaid the  loan
amount, the five cheques in question be returned to him.
</p>

<p id="p_23">12.       Perusal of TCR shows that the appellant remained inconsistent
in   putting   his   defence   while  replying  to   notice  under   Section  251
<a href="/doc/445276/" id="a_13">CrPC</a> in all the five complaint cases. In complaint case bearing CC
NO.4378/1/12, he stated that he borrowed a sum of Rs.20,000/­ from
the   complainant   and   that   he   had   already   repaid   part   amount   of
Rs.16,320/­ leaving unpaid balance amount of Rs.3,680/­. Whereas in
complaint cases bearing CC NO.4380/1/12, 4379/1/12, 4381/1/12 and
4382/1/12, the appellant stated that he borrowed a sum of Rs.5000/­
from   the   appellant   and   that   he   has   already   repaid   the   entire   said
amount to the complainant.
</p>

<p id="p_24">13.       The next defence taken by the appellant is that the stamp paper
on   which   document/   affidavit   Ex.CW1/A   was   prepared,   was
purchased in the year 2010 but the same was used in January 2012 for
preparation   of   affidavit   qua   loan   transaction   and   that   complainant
failed to explain the long gap. Ld. Counsel for the appellant argued
that loan document Ex.CW1/A is not legally valid document in view
CA No.54458­62/16 &amp; 54499/16          Sanjay Shah vs. Narender Budhiraja                              Page10of17
 of the register of Notary Public not having been proved before the Ld.
Trial Court. Ld. Trial Court dealt with these contentions and rightly
observed   in   the   impugned   judgments   which   are   reproduced   as
under :­
                "Ex.CW1/A records that the accused herein obtained a
                loan of Rs.5,24,000/­ for the purpose of purchasing a
                property on 22.10.2011 and agreed to repay the same
                on 20.01.2012. This document bears the signatures of
                the   accused   and   at   the   bottom   it   bears   the   date   of
                execution i.e. 06.01.2012. The stamp present on the
                back   side   of   this   document   clearly   shows   that   the
                stamp   paper   from   the   document   was   purchased   in
                2010 whereas the document itself has been executed
                in   2012.   It   is   also   evident   from   a   perusal   of   the
                document that the deponent i.e. the accused has been
                identified by none other than the complainant himself.
</p>
<p id="p_25">                "In his statement under 313 <a href="/doc/445276/" id="a_14">CrPC</a> as well as in his
                application under section 145(2) NI Act, the accused
                stated that the complainant had taken his signatures on
                a blank stamp paper. The accused has also nowhere
                unequivocally   denied   his   signatures   on   Ex.CW1/A.
                Thus, the signatures of the accused on Ex.CW1/A are
                admitted.  As  the   accused   has   already   admitted   his
                signatures   on   this   document,  therefore   any
                contradiction in the testimony of the complainant with
                respect   to   the   name   of   the   purchaser   of   the   stamp
                paper   or   the   fact   that   the   complainant   does   not
                remember the name of the vendor of the stamp paper
                is not material."
</p>
<p id="p_26">                "Now,   where   a   person   admits   his   signatures   on   a
                document, it is to be presumed that it was filled before
                he signed it. Reliance is placed upon the judgment of
                the Delhi High Court in Mukesh Gupta vs. P.K. Bajaj

CA No.54458­62/16 &amp; 54499/16          Sanjay Shah vs. Narender Budhiraja                              Page11of17
                 and   Anr.  (decision   dated   20.11.2006   in   CS   (OS)
                No.1615/2003).   However,   this   is   a   rebuttable
                presumption and can be dislodged by leading evidence
                to   the   contrary.   Having   said   this,   however,   I   am
                unable to accept that the salient features of Ex.CW1/A
                as   detailed   above   impinge   the   credibility   or   legal
                validity of this document. To begin with, I am unable
                to accept the contention that the  said document has
                been executed on an expired stamp paper. There is no
                provision in the Stamp Act 1899 which prescribes the
                validity period of a stamp paper. Even Section 54 of
                the Stamp Act which has presumably been relied upon
                by the Ld. Counsel only provides that where a stamp
                paper   purchased   has   not   been   used,   then   upon
                presentation of such a stamp paper to the Collector,
                the Collector shall repay the value of the stamp paper
                to   the   purchaser.   I   am   also   supported   in   this
                conclusion by the judgment of the Supreme Court in
                <a href="/doc/14125/" id="a_15">Thiruvengada   Pillai   vs.   Navaneethammal   &amp;   Anr</a>.
                (judgment dated 19.02.2008 of the Supreme Court in
                Writ Petition (civil) 290 of 2001 wherein the Supreme
                Court rejected the objection raised to the validity of a
                sale agreement executed on a stamp paper purchased
                more than six months back."
</p>

<p id="p_27">14.       Further   defence   of   the   appellant   is   that   the   complainant   is
completely silent about the cheques in question in Ex.CW1/A which
fact is sufficient to disbelieve the case of the complainant. 
          Perusal   of   document   Ex.CW1/A   shows   that   it   is   dated
06.01.2012 whereas the cheques in question bear subsequent dates.
Therefore, obviously the particulars of the cheques could not have
been mentioned in Ex.CW1/A.
</p>

<p id="p_28">CA No.54458­62/16 &amp; 54499/16          Sanjay Shah vs. Narender Budhiraja                              Page12of17
</p><p id="p_29"> 15.       Ld. counsel for the appellant argued that the complainant has
not   proved   his   financial   capability   for   advancement   of   huge   loan
amount to the appellant. He further contended that complainant failed
to disclose the source from which he managed such a huge amount of
Rs.5,24,000/­.   He   further   submitted   that   it   is   unbelievable   that
complainant   would   have   advanced   a   huge   loan   amount   to   the
appellant who is a poor weekly market vendor having no capability to
repay   the   same.   He   also   submitted   that   complainant   has   failed   to
produce   his   income­tax   returns   indicating   advancement   of   loan
amount to the complainant in the relevant year. 
          The contention of the appellant/accused that the complainant's
failure to file any income tax returns reflecting the advancement of
the loan in question to the accused is fatal flaw in the complainant's
case can be discarded by placing reliance on the Judgment in Krishna
P. Murajkar Vs. Joe Ferrao­ 2013 ACD 942 Bombay, which is to the
effect:­
                "Any default in paying tax is a matter between the
                defaulter   and   revenue   authorities   and   it   is   not   a
                ground   in   law   to   deny   avenues   open   to   the
                complainant for recovering the loan amount". 
</p>

<p id="p_30">16.       Ld. counsel  for  the complainant emphatically  submitted that
the complaint cases were filed way back in the year 2012 and since
then the appellant has not indicated any bonafide intention to repay

CA No.54458­62/16 &amp; 54499/16          Sanjay Shah vs. Narender Budhiraja                              Page13of17
 the loan amount which is evident from his conduct before the Ld.
Trial Court as well as this Court.                             
</p>

<p id="p_31">17.       Hon'ble Apex Court in case of M/s Laxmi Dyechem v. State of
Gujraj &amp; Ors., Criminal Appeal Nos. 1870­1909 of 2012, decided on
27.11.2012 qua Section 138 of NI Act observed that:
</p><blockquote id="blockquote_1">                   " ... the object underlying the provision contained in the
                   said Chapter was aimed at securing faith in the efficacy
                   of   banking   operations   and   giving   credibility   to
                   negotiable   instruments   in   business   and   day   to   day
                   transactions by making dishonour of such instruments
                   an offence ...".
</blockquote>

<p id="p_32">18.       The Hon'ble Apex Court in case titled State of Tamil Nadu v.
M.K. Kandaswami, (1975) 4 SCC observed that:­
                    "while interpreting a penal provision u/s 138 of <a href="/doc/1132672/" id="a_16">NI
                    Act</a>,   endevour   should   be   made   to   preserve   the
                    workability and efficacy of the statute rather than an
                    interpretation   that   would   render   the   law   otiose   or
                    sterile." 
</p>

<p id="p_33">19.                 In Goa Plast(P) Ltd. Vs. Chico Ursula D'Souza, Hon'ble
Supreme Court, while dealing with the objects of the Act, observed as
follows:­
                  "26...   The   object   and   the   ingredients   under   the
                  provisions, in particular, Sections 138 and 139 of the
                  Act   cannot   be   ignored.     Proper   and   smooth
                  functioning of all business transactions, particularly,
                  of  cheques  as  instruments,  primarily  depends  upon

CA No.54458­62/16 &amp; 54499/16          Sanjay Shah vs. Narender Budhiraja                              Page14of17
                   the   integrity   and   honesty   of   the   parties.     In   our
                  country,   in   a   large   number   of   commercial
                  transactions,   it   was   noted   that   the   cheques   were
                  issued even merely as a device not only to stall but
                  even   to   defraud   the   creditors.     The   sanctity   and
                  credibility   of   issuance   of   cheques   in   commercial
                  transactions   was   eroded   to   a   large   extent.
</p><p id="p_34">                  Undoubtedly,   dishonour   of   a   cheque   by   the   bank
                  causes incalculable loss, injury and inconvenience to
                  the payee and the entire credibility of the business
                  transactions within and outside the country suffers a
                  serious setback.   Parliament, in order to restore the
                  credibility of cheques as a trustworthy substitute for
                  cash payment enacted the aforesaid   provisions.  The
                  remedy   available   in   a   civil   court   is   a   long   drawn
                  matter   and   unscrupulous   drawer   normally   take s
                  various   pleas   to   defeat   the   genuine   claim   of   the
                  payee."
</p>



<p id="p_35">20.       Admittedly, the loan was taken in the year 2011. Instead of
making efforts to pay back the consideration, appellant tried every
trick in the trade to retain unjust enrichment. He has been able to
procrastinate   the   proceedings   by   taking   false   &amp;   frivolous   pleas   to
avoid repayment of the loan taken by him. He has not shown any
bonafide intention to make the payments towards the loan taken in
since the year 2011 and even after filing of the complaint cases. The
conduct of the appellant is, therefore, not in consonance with the aim
and objective of the Act as discussed above in <a href="/doc/193064100/" id="a_17">M/s Laxmi Dyechem v.
State of Gujraj &amp; Ors</a>. (supra), Goa Plast(P) Ltd. Vs. Chico Ursula


CA No.54458­62/16 &amp; 54499/16          Sanjay Shah vs. Narender Budhiraja                              Page15of17
 D'Souza   (supra)   and   State   of   Tamil   Nadu   v.   M.K.   Kandaswami
(supra). 
</p>

<p id="p_36">21.       Keeping   in   view   the   said   aim   of   the   Act   and   the   fact   that
defence   put   forth   by   the   appellant   lacks   credibility,   I   endorse   the
conviction recorded by the Ld. Trial Court rejecting the defence put
up by the appellant. In view of the foregoing reasons, the aforesaid
appeals filed by the appellant are dismissed. 
</p>

<p id="p_37">22.       Now coming to appeal bearing CA No. 54499/16 vide which
the complainant has assailed the orders on sentence dated 16.05.2016
on   the   ground   that   lesser   punishment   has   been   awarded   to   the
respondent. It is submitted on behalf of the complainant that having
prosecuted the cases for over more than four years, the complainant
has remained deprived of his money. As per Section 138 of the Act,
the accused can be punished with imprisonment for a term which may
be extended to two years or with fine which may extend to twice the
amount of the cheque or with both. 
</p>

<p id="p_38">23.       Keeping in view the facts and circumstances of the case and
the aim and objectives of the Act, it is held that Ld. Trial Court has
already granted a reasonable sentence and compensation in all the
aforesaid five complaint cases. Accordingly, the appeal bearing CA
No.54499/16 filed by the complainant is also dismissed. 
</p>

cases.
